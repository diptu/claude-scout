# Engineering Manager Playbook

Helps engineering managers (and engineers moving into management) handle the recurring situations of the job: running effective 1:1s, giving feedback, conducting performance reviews, hiring and interviewing, structuring career growth, managing team health, and handling difficult conversations. Draw on this skill whenever a user's request is about *leading or managing people on an engineering team*, as opposed to writing or reviewing code.

## When to apply this skill

Apply this skill when the user asks for help with things like:
- Preparing for or running a 1:1 with a direct report
- Writing or reviewing a performance review or feedback
- Structuring a promotion case or career growth conversation
- Designing an interview loop or evaluating a candidate
- Handling a difficult personnel situation (underperformance, conflict, burnout)
- Building team rituals (retros, standups, on-call rotations, sprint planning)
- Thinking through org design, delegation, or scaling a team
- Onboarding a new engineer or new manager

Do not apply this skill to purely technical questions (architecture, debugging, code review) unless the user explicitly frames it as a management or people question.

## Core approach

1. **Clarify the situation before prescribing advice.** Ask (or infer from context) who is involved, the stakes, and what's already been tried. Management advice that ignores context is generic and unhelpful — tailor the response to the specific team size, seniority of the report, and company stage the user describes.

2. **Separate the message from the mechanism.** Most management tasks have two parts: *what* needs to be communicated (the substance — feedback, a decision, expectations) and *how* it's delivered (the format — a 1:1 agenda, a written review, a Slack message). Help with both, but get the substance right first; a well-structured document with the wrong message is worse than a rough one with the right message.

3. **Default to specific, behavioral language over vague traits.** When drafting feedback, performance review content, or promotion cases, favor concrete observations ("missed three sprint commitments in Q2, each without early flagging") over abstract judgments ("isn't reliable"). This is more actionable for the report and more defensible in calibration.

4. **Surface trade-offs, don't just pick one answer.** Management decisions (how to handle an underperformer, whether to promote, how to structure a team) usually have a real trade-off between competing goods (speed vs. fairness, individual vs. team needs, short-term delivery vs. long-term growth). Name the trade-off explicitly rather than presenting one option as obviously correct.

## Step-by-step guidance by task type

### 1:1s
- Ask what's been on the report's mind recently, not just status updates — 1:1s work best as the report's time, not a status meeting.
- Suggest a lightweight recurring agenda: wins/blockers, career/growth check-in, feedback in both directions, and anything time-sensitive.
- For a specific difficult 1:1 (giving hard feedback, addressing a pattern), help the user script an opening line that states the observation plainly and invites response, rather than burying it in praise or hedging.

### Performance reviews and feedback
- Ground every claim in specific, dated examples, not general impressions.
- Balance strengths and growth areas; avoid recency bias by asking the user to think across the whole review period, not just the last few weeks.
- For growth areas, pair each one with a concrete next step or experiment, not just a label.

### Promotion cases
- Frame the case around the target level's expectations, not just "this person is good" — map specific accomplishments to the specific competencies of the next level.
- Flag gaps honestly rather than glossing over them; a promotion case that ignores an obvious gap is more likely to fail in calibration.

### Hiring and interviewing
- Help define what the role actually needs before drafting questions — a generic interview loop tests generic skills, not the ones that matter for this role.
- For technical interviews, favor questions that reveal how a candidate thinks and communicates under uncertainty over trivia recall.
- After an interview, help the user write feedback that's specific enough for someone who wasn't in the room to understand the signal.

### Team health and rituals
- When a user describes a team ritual that isn't working (standups that drag, retros nobody engages with), diagnose the likely root cause (wrong audience, wrong cadence, no follow-through on action items) before proposing a fix.
- For on-call and workload questions, weigh sustainability explicitly — a rotation that burns people out will cost more in attrition than it saves in coverage.

### Difficult conversations and underperformance
- Help the user separate the pattern from a single incident — one-off events rarely warrant formal action, but ask whether this fits a pattern the user has already flagged before.
- Suggest documenting what was discussed and agreed after any hard conversation, so expectations are traceable later.
- Remind the user that legal/HR involvement may be needed for formal performance plans or terminations — this skill can help structure the conversation and language, not substitute for that process.

### New or first-time managers
- When the user signals they're new to management, default to more foundational framing (what a 1:1 is for, why written feedback matters) rather than assuming familiarity with management practice.
- Normalize that management is a distinct skill from the engineering work that got them promoted — encourage treating it as something to deliberately practice, not something that should come naturally immediately.
